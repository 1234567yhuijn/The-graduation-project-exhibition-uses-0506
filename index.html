Dialogue with Generation Z: Becoming “Nezha” or “Soldier A”

Dialogue with Generation Z | Who Am I: Neither Demon Nor Immortal

By Yun Liuyi in Beijing | China Daily Global | Updated: 2025-05-08 10:10

 

Editor’s Note At the start of 2025, the film Nezha: Mo tong nao hai exploded onto the market, soared into the global box office’s top five and ranked first among Chinese films. Following Sun Wukong, Nezha has once again fueled public interest in China’s mythological rebellion narratives. Now that Gen Z has become the largest consumer group for popular culture products, their rebellious spirit, as exemplified by the phrase "My fate is determined by me, not heaven," seems to have faded. Instead, they are often labeled as "Buddhist," "slacking," and "lying flat." Pop culture draws on life and reflects it; its content maps the emotions and attitudes of Gen Z. On one hand, rebellious narratives gain favor; on the other, “burning incense and praying” has become increasingly popular. What is the true face of Generation Z? Will they choose to become Nezha or merely “Soldier A”? In this installment, we explore their world through the lens of globally hot pop-culture products.

In the movie, as Nezha participates in the Celestial Examination, he gradually uncovers the hypocrisy of the deities, shattering his idealized perception of them As Generation Z matures, they too feel life’s pressures. The surprise hit Chinese drama of 2024, To the Wonder, and the award-winning 2020 British series Normal People both open with rebellion, yet neither concludes with the protagonist reclaiming everything.

This reflects another facet of Gen Z’s psyche. After intense debates on #meritocracy , Gen Z is abandoning traditional measures of success and seeking a more self-consistent way of life—on a weekday afternoon, Xiaolin reels in his line at sea in the East China Sea; on Friday nights, Qiqi sheds her work badge and dons a bartender’s uniform to shake cocktails; one year into her job, Eli hands in her resignation and sets out to find a new path...

“An aspirational drama’s framework, a prose poem’s narrative”

A few years ago, Xiaolin graduated in photography from China’s second-ranked performing-arts university. Rather than “pursuing art,” he joined a hospital’s publicity department, where beyond shooting promotional videos and ID photos of doctors, his work scarcely involves “images.” Many see this job as a dead end. Yet Xiaolin finds joy in it. With a light workload, he devotes extra time to activities such as deep-sea fishing, motorcycling, and playing badminton.

Friends jest that he radiates “supreme relaxation.” Xiaolin says he freed himself from society’s definition of success long ago—around middle school, when he and classmates were punished by standing in the corridor. While he fidgeted, a peer leaned comfortably against the wall, gazing out the window at the sky. In that empty corridor, illuminated by blue sky, sunlight, birds, and the scent of flowers, he realized the punishment was trivial. From then on, he chose to live in the moment, defining himself by life’s simple happiness. “I don’t want to shackle myself. Sure, I get anxious sometimes—but who knows what the future holds? All we can grasp is today’s joy and contentment,” he says.

In 2024, the eight-episode mini-series To the Wonder stunned viewers. Its premiere episode topped its time slot, and it now holds an 8.9 rating on Douban,China’s Rotten Tomatoes, ranking second among 2024 Chinese dramas. The series was also selected for the competition segment of the 7th Canneseries International Series Festival. After the show’s release, Altay became a dream destination for viewers, with tourism bookings surging by 562% within a week. Unlike Nezha 2’s rebellion, To the Wonder constructs a spiritual utopia.

The heroine, Li Wenxiu, a middle-school dropout dreaming of becoming a writer, works at a hotel in Urumqi and sneaks into writing lectures in her spare time. Ridiculed for her “literary dreams,” fired, and scammed out of her severance pay, penniless Wenxiu returns to Altai to find her mother. Rather than spitting vengeance or returning triumphantly to Urumqi, she discovers in Altai’s simple life that the wellspring of writing lies in “loving, living, and suffering.” Amid mountains, rivers, and seas, she finds the life she wants and resonates with her writing.

At 24, Eli once saw herself as the heroine of a rebellion narrative. After graduating from university, she chose a high-paying job that held no interest for her. "At the time, I didn’t think much about it. I had complete confidence in myself, believing I could force myself to accomplish anything," Eli recalled her thought process when making that choice. However, after working for a while, Eli found that the work hours were proportional to her income, completely draining her personal life. The mental stress of long hours and investing all her time in a job she disliked began to take a toll on her physical and mental well-being.

After careful consideration, Eli decided to cut her losses and leave the company. Now, Eli has temporarily freed herself from her previous relentless pace. She feels she needs to contemplate her future direction and explore her true interests instead of being in such a hurry to "control life." Eli has started learning photography, maintaining her reading habit, and trying her hand at writing. Her days are peaceful but filled with a sense of slowly flowing happiness.

“No Talk of the Future, Only Control of the Present”

Besides To the Wonder, the 2020 British series Normal People charts a path of self-reconciliation. It opens in a rural Irish high school: Marianne, from a wealthy yet cold family, endures her brother’s verbal abuse; Connell, the popular athlete, hides his poverty and fears its taint on his reputation. Despite opposite backgrounds, both feel unworthy of love. Marianne believes she is ugly and undeserving of love, while Connell fears that his family background will affect how others perceive him, so he suppresses his true self. They gravitate toward each other, but Connell’s timidity drives Marianne away.

At Trinity College Dublin, their fortunes reverse: Marianne’s flair and privilege let her sail through social circles; Connell, out of his depth, becomes marginalized. Yet they remain drawn together, enduring breakups and reconciliations. Connell battles depression; Marianne escapes unhealthy relationships, seeking validation through pain. Through turmoil, they bolster each other—Connell reassures Marianne “I love you; you owe me nothing,” and she encourages him to pursue writing.

They influence and heal each other, and ultimately both overcome their socially conditioned selves, taking brave steps—Connell receives a master’s offer from an American university and bravely steps out into the world; Marianne chooses to stay in Ireland, bravely remaining behind. Both achieve self-reconciliation and mutual redemption.

Like Marianne and Connell, Qiqi, a Gen Z, once fell into the anxiety of peer pressure and others’ evaluations.Qiqi hails from a harmonious but somewhat traditional Chinese family where her mother hoped she would become an "excellent" individual. Qiqi initially strove to meet her mother’s expectations. Everything went smoothly at first—she was an outstanding student from a young age and successfully gained admission to a master’s program after her undergraduate studies. Qiqi nonetheless found her dream industry—advertising.However, she soon realized that the advertising industry values work experience over academic qualifications, giving an edge to peers with two more years of experience.

Rejecting higher salaries in tech, she risked an internship at a renowned consultancy, only to face grueling hours, travel, and sleep deprivation. Once physically and mentally battered, she redefined success: living by overdrawing now for future gain shortens life; balance suits her better. Post-graduation, she joined a startup, trading pay for fulfillment—but after three months, layoffs struck. Though reluctant, this departure reinforced her understanding that many aspects of life are beyond one’s control

“When I realized many things are beyond my control, I chose to control what I can: my present life and what brings me comfort. That present fuels my excitement and drive for the next steps. I want more of these moments, so I strive onward,” Qiqi says.

China’s one-child policy and longer economic boom gave Gen Z confidence to seek self beyond traditional success. Reflection on “performance-ism” is a hot topic; having grown amid competition, they now grasp that effort doesn’t always yield reward. Perhaps ‘do your best and leave the rest to fate’ is life’s best attitude. Even an ordinary life offers ordinary happiness. Amid the noise, Gen Z affirms multiple success paths. Between demonhood and immortality, they choose to be themselves.
